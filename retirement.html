Roth conversions: where the circuit stops being convex.

On a working-age 1040, “least tax” is a small convex program — a line search down the brackets. In retirement it isn’t. Two documented mechanisms — the Social Security torpedo and the IRMAA Medicare cliffs — make the cost of converting a dollar to Roth rise, then fall, then jump. The objective is non-convex and discontinuous, so finding the best conversion needs a mixed-integer program (one year) and a dynamic program (across years).

Why a line search breaks

The working-age circuit has one nonlinear edge — the bracket function — and it is convex: the marginal rate only ever goes up as income rises. So filling the lowest available bracket first is optimal, and “optimize my levers” is a tiny linear program. A retiree converting a traditional IRA to Roth faces a different cost surface. The conversion is ordinary income, but it drags two other quantities with it:

  • torpedo Taxable Social Security. As provisional income rises, each conversion dollar can pull $0.50 then $0.85 of Social Security into taxable income (Pub. 915, Worksheet 1). For a while a dollar converted adds $1.85 of taxable income — the marginal rate humps up — then, once 85% of benefits are taxed, the extra stops and the rate falls back. A hump up-then-down is not convex.
  • cliff IRMAA. Medicare Part B and D premiums step up at fixed MAGI thresholds (Federal Register 2024‑26474). Crossing a threshold by one dollar adds a full year’s surcharge at once — a discontinuous jump of hundreds to thousands of dollars.
  • stacking Capital-gains stacking. Long-term gains and qualified dividends sit on top of ordinary income (the Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet). A conversion can push gains from the 0% band into the 15% band, adding rate on income that isn’t even the conversion.

The circuit

The working-age 1040 is a combinational circuit — a feed-forward graph with two small internal feedback loops. The retirement version is a closed-loop control system: a non-convex one-year tax block wrapped in a feedback loop that runs across years. The IRA balance is the state; the Roth conversion is the control you use to bend it.

Inner block (top): one year’s tax is feed-forward — income and Social Security pass through the SS worksheet, the brackets, and the IRMAA step to a stage cost. Three edges are the troublemakers: the Social Security torpedo and gains stacking are non-convex, and the IRMAA step is discontinuous — which is why one year is a MILP. Outer loop (bottom): the balance Bₜ is a state register; required distributions feed back as forced income, the conversion cₜ is the control that drains the balance in low-rate years, and growth ×(1+g) with a one-year delay z⁻¹ close the loop. Minimizing lifetime tax over that loop is the dynamic program. (In practice IRMAA reacts to MAGI from two years prior — a z⁻² delay on that branch; the model uses same-year for clarity.)

One year: a mixed-integer linear program

The continuous part of the cost — brackets, the Social-Security ramp, capital-gains stacking, NIIT — is piecewise-linear but non-convex. The IRMAA part is a discontinuous step. We model the conversion c exactly: the continuous cost as a piecewise-linear function with a breakpoint at every kink (an SOS2 / convex-combination representation, with weights \(\lambda_i\) on the kink values), and the IRMAA step with one binary \(z_k\) per MAGI tier, switched by big-\(M\) constraints — the same device the 1040 model uses for the Saver’s-Credit step.

$$\begin{aligned} \max_{c,\ \lambda,\ z}\quad & r^{*}\, c \;-\;\Big(\textstyle\sum_i \lambda_i\,T_i \;+\; \sum_k s_k\, z_k \;-\; \text{cost}(0)\Big)\\[2pt] \text{s.t.}\quad & c=\textstyle\sum_i \lambda_i\, c_i,\qquad \textstyle\sum_i \lambda_i = 1,\qquad \lambda \ \text{SOS2 (only adjacent }\lambda_i>0)\\ & \textstyle\sum_k z_k = 1,\qquad z_k\in\{0,1\}\\ & L_k - M(1-z_k)\ \le\ \textstyle\sum_i \lambda_i\,\mathrm{MAGI}_i\ \le\ U_k + M(1-z_k)\quad\forall k \end{aligned}$$

Here \(c_i\) are the conversion amounts at the kinks, \(T_i\) the continuous tax there, \(s_k\) the annual IRMAA surcharge in tier \(k\) with MAGI band \([L_k,U_k]\), and \(r^{*}\) the future marginal rate the conversion avoids. The big-\(M\) rows force the active tier to match the chosen MAGI, so the binary truly prices the cliff. Solved with scipy.optimize.milp (HiGHS), this recovers the global optimum — verified to match a fine line search to the dollar across \(r^{*}=20\%\ldots30\%\). Across years: a dynamic program

The single-year problem already needs binaries. Stringing years together adds state: the traditional-IRA balance grows, and from age 73 it throws off Required Minimum Distributions — forced taxable income equal to the prior year-end balance divided by an age factor (Pub. 590-B, Uniform Lifetime Table). A conversion today is taxed now but shrinks every future RMD. Years are coupled; you cannot optimize one in isolation.

Enumerating conversion schedules is hopeless: \(G\) choices per year over \(T\) years is \(G^{T}\) policies — for \(G=81,\ T=25\) that is about \(10^{48}\). Dynamic programming over the one-dimensional balance state is \(T\cdot|B|\cdot G\) — here about 326,000 stage evaluations. Each stage solves the same non-convex single-year cost; Bellman’s recursion stitches them into an optimal schedule. (Validated against brute force on a short horizon: 0.14% gap.)

Concretely, the state is the traditional-IRA balance \(B_t\) at age \(a_t=a_0+t\); the control is the conversion \(c_t\). The value function (present-value cost-to-go) satisfies the Bellman recursion

$$\begin{aligned} J_T(B) &= \rho\, B,\\[2pt] J_t(B) &= \min_{0\,\le\, c\,\le\, B-R(B,a_t)}\;\Big\{\; g_t(B,c)\;+\;\beta\, J_{t+1}\big(\underbrace{(B-R(B,a_t)-c)\,(1+g)}_{B_{t+1}}\big)\;\Big\},\\[4pt] \pi_t^{*}(B) &= \arg\min_{c}\big\{\,g_t(B,c)+\beta\, J_{t+1}(B_{t+1})\,\big\},\qquad \text{objective }=\,J_0(B_0). \end{aligned}$$

Here \(R(B,a)=B/\delta(a)\) is the required minimum distribution — zero before age 73, with \(\delta(a)\) the Uniform Lifetime divisor (Pub. 590-B); \(g_t(B,c)=\mathrm{Tax}\big(\text{pension}+R(B,a_t)+c\big)\) is the non-convex, discontinuous single-year cost — the MILP above is exactly the inner \(\min\) over \(c\); \(\beta=1/(1+d)\) is the discount factor, \(g\) the growth rate, and \(\rho\) the assumed rate on any balance left at the horizon. The inner block (one year, a MILP) and the outer loop (Bellman over the balance) are the two layers of the circuit.

Doing nothing lets the IRA grow to $1.38M; after age 73 its RMDs are forced out at rising rates, and a six-figure deferred liability rides to the end. The optimizer converts hard in the low-rate early years — each year stopping just under an IRMAA threshold — draining the balance by age 80, after which that retiree’s tax is just $1,628/yr. The result is sensitive to the assumed future/heir rate: that terminal condition is part of the control problem, not a hidden constant.

That chart is one trajectory — the path the optimal policy takes from this particular $900k start. The DP actually produces the whole feedback policy \(\pi^{*}_t(B)\): for any balance at any age, how much to convert. Here is that policy surface, with the trajectory above drawn on top of it.

Read a column as “at this age, given this balance, convert this much.” The policy is increasing in the balance (more in the IRA → convert more) and shifts at age 73 when RMDs begin to pre-empt part of the draw. The blue trajectory shows the realized path sliding down through the field as conversions drain the balance to zero by age 80 — after which the policy correctly calls for no conversions (nothing left to convert). The banding within a column is the cliff-aware behavior: conversions cluster at amounts that stop just under an IRMAA threshold.

So the same idea that turned a 1040 into a circuit scales: the forward map is the documented tax engine; “least tax” is an optimization over the levers. The levers here — how much to convert, and when — live in a cost landscape with humps and cliffs, which is exactly why it takes a mixed-integer program and a dynamic program rather than a line down the brackets.
