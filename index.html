educational tax model

Your Form 1040 is a circuit.

A tax return is a directed graph of signed arithmetic with two feedback loops. Once you see it that way, “lowering your effective rate” becomes a concrete question: which inputs are levers, and how far do they move the output. This page explains the circuit and lets you run it — including the two cycles, solved live as fixed points.

Not tax advice. This is an educational model of the 2025 Form 1040 with simplified assumptions (see Methodology & sources). Figures are illustrative and may differ from your actual return. Consult a qualified tax professional before acting.

The shape of the computation

Number the lines of Form 1040 and Schedules 1–3 and you get a directed acyclic graph: every line references only earlier-numbered lines, so the form fills top-to-bottom in one pass. The line numbering is a topological sort. Each edge carries a signed operation:

  • Σ add income lines sum into line 9 (every parent +1).
  • − subtract adjustments, the deduction, and credits enter as negative parents (AGI = 9 − 10, taxable = 11 − 14).
  • × rate exactly one nonlinear edge: taxable → tax, the convex piecewise-linear bracket function.
  • clamp credits and refund/owe carry max(·,0) limiters.

A DAG plus two cycles

Treat each cyclic pair as a single super-node and the whole thing is a clean DAG — its condensation. Zoom into the two red super-nodes and you find the only places acyclicity breaks: two strongly connected components, each a pair of lines that reference each other, solved by iterating to a fixed point. Each converges because its slope is a product of tax rates below 1 (loop gain < 1).

Where the levers are

Most inputs are facts (your wages, your withholding). A handful are levers — choices that move the output without changing your underlying income. Their value is the marginal effect on tax, read straight off the signed edges:

deduction

Standard vs. itemized

Take the larger. With only a W-2 and no big deductible expenses, the standard deduction ($15,750 single / $31,500 MFJ for 2025) almost always wins.

adjustment

Traditional IRA / HSA

Deductible contributions lower AGI dollar-for-dollar, so each dollar saves your marginal rate (10–37¢ on the dollar). Bounded by the contribution caps.

credit

Saver’s Credit

At lower incomes a retirement contribution also earns a 50/20/10% nonrefundable credit — so the first dollars can return ~60¢ each (bracket + credit). It phases out by ~$40k (single).

status

Filing status

Sets the standard deduction and the bracket cutoffs. A fact for most, but it indexes every threshold in the circuit.

The second loop, live: self-employed health ↔ premium tax credit

For a self-employed filer with a Marketplace plan, the §162(l) health-insurance deduction and the premium tax credit each depend on the other through MAGI. This solves the SE-health ↔ PTC fixed point (Pub. 974) and contrasts it with the tempting one-pass that gets it wrong.

Effective rates across incomes

Run a single filer (standard deduction, W-2 only) across the income range and the effective federal rate traces the familiar progressive curve — from about 7% at $40k of wages to ~21% at $250k, bending gently at each bracket boundary. The levers shift you down this curve; the calculator finds how far.

It never closes — everyone saves something — but never reaches zero, because every wage here is above the point where contributions can fully offset the tax.

One honest framing: the $4.49M “saved” is tax reduced by routing $10,800 per filer into your own tax-advantaged accounts — deferral, not free cash. The only genuinely free-lunch piece, the Saver’s Credit, touches just 4.8% of this high-income cohort; on this dataset the deduction does the work.

How it works

The forward graph is a function tax = F(inputs, levers). The two feedback loops are solved by iterating a map to a fixed point — each a contraction because its slope is a product of tax rates below 1. “Least tax” is then an optimization over the lever variables: a small mixed-integer linear program whose constraints are the graph’s affine edges, with the convex bracket function as the one nonlinearity. The full derivation, the exact constants, and the IRS sources are on the Methodology & sources page.
