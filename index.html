educational tax model

Your Form 1040 is a circuit.

A tax return is a directed graph of signed arithmetic with two feedback loops. Once you see it that way, “lowering your effective rate” becomes a concrete question: which inputs are levers, and how far do they move the output. This page explains the circuit and lets you run it.

Not tax advice. This is an educational model of the 2025 Form 1040 with simplified assumptions (see Methodology). Figures are illustrative and may differ from your actual return. Consult a qualified tax professional before acting.

The shape of the computation

Number the lines of Form 1040 and Schedules 1–3 and you get a directed acyclic graph: every line references only earlier-numbered lines, so the form fills top-to-bottom in one pass. The line numbering is a topological sort. Each edge carries a signed operation:

  • Σ add income lines sum into line 9 (every parent +1).
  • − subtract adjustments, the deduction, and credits enter as negative parents (AGI = 9 − 10, taxable = 11 − 14).
  • × rate exactly one nonlinear edge: taxable → tax, the convex piecewise-linear bracket function.
  • clamp credits and refund/owe carry max(·,0) limiters.

The forward path is a summing chain; the two red blocks are the only cycles — each a pair of lines that reference each other, iterated to a fixed point. Everything else is a single top-to-bottom pass.

Where the levers are

Most inputs are facts (your wages, your withholding). A handful are levers — choices that move the output without changing your underlying income. Their value is the marginal effect on tax, read straight off the signed edges:

deduction

Standard vs. itemized

Take the larger. With only a W-2 and no big deductible expenses, the standard deduction ($15,750 single / $31,500 MFJ for 2025) almost always wins.

adjustment

Traditional IRA / HSA

Deductible contributions lower AGI dollar-for-dollar, so each dollar saves your marginal rate (10–37¢ on the dollar). Bounded by the contribution caps.

credit

Saver’s Credit

At lower incomes a retirement contribution also earns a 50/20/10% nonrefundable credit — so the first dollars can return ~60¢ each (bracket + credit). It phases out by ~$40k (single).

status

Filing status

Sets the standard deduction and the bracket cutoffs. A fact for most, but it indexes every threshold in the circuit.

How it works (the math)

The forward graph is a function tax = F(inputs, levers). Two refinements make it exact:

The two feedback loops are fixed points

Two pairs of lines reference each other and cannot be ordered away: the IRA deduction ↔ taxable Social Security coupling, and the self-employed-health deduction ↔ premium tax credit coupling. Each is solved by iterating a map x ← G(x) to a fixed point. Both converge because the map’s slope is a product of tax rates < 1 — a contraction (loop gain below 1), so the iteration is stable and lands in a handful of steps. Contract those two loops and what remains is a clean DAG.

Minimizing tax is an optimization over the levers

“Least tax” is min F over the lever variables subject to the contribution caps and eligibility guards. Because the only nonlinearity is the convex bracket function, this is a small mixed-integer linear program: the graph’s affine edges become linear constraints, the lever inputs become decision variables, and the bracket map becomes a convex piecewise-linear relaxation. The calculator above uses a fast grid search over the same feasible set.

Sources & assumptions

  • 2025 Form 1040 & Schedules 1–3 line arithmetic; the “Keep for Your Records” worksheets (Tax Computation, Social Security Benefits, Standard Deduction).
  • 2025 standard deduction and rate schedule; Saver’s Credit (Form 8880) tiers; IRA phase-out (Pub. 590-A); premium tax credit applicable percentages (Rev. Proc. 2024-35, Form 8962); SE-health ↔ PTC iteration (Pub. 974).
  • Simplifications: the calculator assumes the standard deduction, full IRA deductibility (no active-participant phase-out), and no capital-gains preferential rates; the two fixed-point loops are documented but not run inside this basic calculator. These are exactly the edge cases the full model’s fixed-point solvers handle.
