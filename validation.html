Checked against NBER TAXSIM

The whole circuit rests on one engine: a function from income to federal tax, built line-by-line from IRS publications. The natural question is whether it’s right. Here it is checked, case by case, against TAXSIM35 — the reference-grade tax microsimulation model maintained by Dan Feenberg at the National Bureau of Economic Research and used across public-finance research.

What had to be matched, and how

Two practical constraints shaped the test:

  • The public taxsim35 endpoint (version 03/22/24) implements federal law only through 2023 — it rejects 2024 and 2025. So the comparison is run at 2023: our engine’s brackets, standard deduction, and long-term-gains breakpoints are set to their 2023 values, while the Social-Security “torpedo” thresholds ($25k/$32k, $34k/$44k) and the NIIT thresholds ($200k/$250k) are statutory and year-invariant, so they are left exactly as the engine ships for 2025.
  • TAXSIM computes federal & state income tax plus FICA, but not Medicare IRMAA. So we compare federal income tax with IRMAA switched off (income tax + NIIT) against TAXSIM’s fiitax, alongside AGI and taxable Social Security.

The battery exercises the parts most likely to hide a bug: the Social-Security torpedo at the 50% and 85% inclusion tiers, capital-gains stacking across the 0% / 15% / 20% breakpoints, the NIIT surtax, the top bracket, and both single and joint filers.

The one discrepancy is TAXSIM’s, not ours

The six differences scale precisely as $100 × bracket (single) or $200 × bracket (joint) — the signature of a standard-deduction gap. They trace entirely to the additional standard deduction for being 65+: our engine uses $1,850 (single) / $1,500 (married, per person); this TAXSIM build used $1,750 / $1,400 — the 2022 figures. The IRS 2023 inflation procedure is explicit:

“… the additional standard deduction amount under § 63(f) for the aged or the blind is $1,500. The additional standard deduction amount is increased to $1,850 if the individual is also unmarried and not a surviving spouse.”
— IRS Rev. Proc. 2022-38, § 2.15 (2023 amounts)

So our value is the correct 2023 figure. As a final check, the harness re-runs with our add-on forced to TAXSIM’s 2022 value — and all 15 cases then match to $0, confirming the add-on is the sole source of the residual. Everything else — AGI assembly, the entire Social-Security torpedo, capital-gains stacking, the NIIT, and the bracket schedule — agrees with TAXSIM to the dollar.

What this does and doesn’t cover

  • Covered & confirmed: the federal income-tax core — ordinary brackets, standard deduction, taxable Social Security (Pub. 915), capital-gains stacking (the QDCGT worksheet), and the NIIT (Form 8960) — reproduces an independent reference implementation exactly.
  • Not covered by TAXSIM: Medicare IRMAA, the discontinuity that makes the retirement problem non-convex, is outside TAXSIM’s scope; it remains validated only against the Federal Register table. And the live 2025 parameters can’t be tested on this endpoint (2023 is its ceiling) — the logic is identical; only the indexed constants differ, each cited to its IRS source.

Reproduce it

The comparison is a single script that POSTs the battery to TAXSIM and diffs it against the engine:

cd models && python3 taxsim_compare.py

Source: models/taxsim_compare.py. It reparameterizes the engine to 2023, submits 15 cases over HTTP, and prints the table above plus the add-on confirmation.
